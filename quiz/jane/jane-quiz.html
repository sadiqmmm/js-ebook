Case Study 1: Jane

Jane is a 30 year old woman, married, gravida 1 para 0 at 41 3/7 weeks. She has no relevant medical history and no past surgeries. Current Obstetric History: Ultrasound. Nonstress test at 40 1/7 weeks was reactive after 40 minutes. Jane missed a nonstress test two days ago due to her car breaking down and no other options for transportation. Induction scheduled.

On admission, Jane tells her nurse that she has not felt her baby move today, and she is concerned because her baby usually moves frequently and noticeably. The nurse applies the external EFM components and the tracing from 0932 represents Jane's initial tracing.

Vaginal examination reveals Jane is 2 cm/50%/-1 station, cephalic presentation, and her membranes ruptured spontaneously during the vaginal examination with thick meconium present in amniotic fluid, moderate amount and no vaginal bleeding. Jane's nurse performed fetal scalp stimulation during the examination, but there was no FHR acceleration in response. Jane's vital signs: 120/78, temp 97.4, pulse 84, respirations 20. The nurse verifies the uterine contractions as indicated on the tracing by palpation and determines that the intensity of contractions is mild, and the resting tone is soft between contractions.

Answer: No.

4) What is the frequency of the contractions?

Answer: 3-4 ½ minutes . Contraction frequency is measured from the beginning of one contraction to the beginning of the next and is expressed in minutes.

5) What is the duration of the contractions?

Answer: 80-110 seconds - Duration is the length of the contraction and is described in seconds.

6) What is the category?

Answer: Category II, indeterminate - Category 2 tracing- Although this tracing is highly concerning and indicates an urgent need for supportive care and assessment by the primary provider due to the clinical picture of absent variability and decreased fetal movement, it is not a category III tracing. Category III criteria are absent variability with recurrent late or recurrent variable decelerations or bradycardia, OR a sinusoidal pattern.

What clinical interventions and communications are appropriate in the presence of these tracing characteristics and the assessment information about Jane?

8) Is internal fetal monitoring needed (FSE or IUPC)?

Answer: No - The external monitor is recording a good fetal tracing that can be evaluated. An interpretable fetal tracing is important for the assessment of fetal status so that the need for uterine resuscitation can be determined. The least invasive mode of monitoring should be used. External monitoring should be adjusted to obtain an interpretable tracing; internal monitoring is generally not needed. Administration of an amnioinfusion requires an IUPC.

9) Change the mother's position?

Answer: Yes - When the mother is in a lateral position (right or left) another non-supine position the uterus does not compress the vena cava or aorta; thus, maternal cardiac return and cardiac output are maximized, and blood flow to the uterus is optimal.

10) Fluid bolus of at least 500 mL of lactated Ringers solution if not contraindicated?

Answer: Yes - Jane has no risk factors that would preclude the bolus administration; her intake and output should be taken into consideration. The nurse should start an IV as soon as possible and administer the fluid bolus. There are data to suggest that increasing IV fluids will positively affect uterine blood flow and thus fetal oxygenation, even in women who are normotensive and well hydrated.

11) Administer supplemental oxygen at 10 L/min using non-rebreather mask and discontinue as soon as possible?

Answer: Yes - Because Jane’s tracing characteristics include absent variability on admission, it is appropriate to administer oxygen at 10L/minute via non-rebreather face mask rather than waiting for response to the less invasive interventions. Discontinue oxygen as soon as possible based on the fetal response.

12) Decrease or discontinue oxytocin?

Answer: No - Jane’s induction has been postponed. When oxygen is chosen for intrauterine resuscitation, it is assumed that other sources of potential fetal physiologic stress have been minimized; thus, oxytocin should not be infusing concurrently with maternal oxygen administration.

13) Administer tocolytic (e.g., terbutaline)?

Answer: No - Tocolytics may be administered as a temporary intrauterine resuscitation measure to reduce uterine activity when tachysystole occurs.

14) Perform fetal scalp stimulation?

Answer: No - The nurse just performed fetal scalp stimulation and there was no fetal response (acceleration). Fetal scalp stimulation is an indirect method used to evaluate oxygenation and acid-base status. An acceleration is associated with fetal-well-being; however, absence of acceleration does not predict fetal compromise. Fetal scalp stimulation is performed during segments of baseline, not during decelerations.

15) Correct maternal hypotension?

Answer: Yes - Hypotension is not present at this time. Hypotension decreases uteroplacental perfusion which becomes readily apparent in the fetal heart rate.

16) Administer a vasopressor (e.g., ephedrine)?

Answer: No - Ephedrine may be administered when a mother has hypotension secondary to regional analgesia or anesthesia.

17) Administer amnioinfusion?

Answer: No - There are no variable decelerations at this time. Amnioinfusion may be administered in an attempt to resolve variable decelerations. These decelerations are often caused by umbilical cord compression due to decreased amniotic fluid.

18) Notify primary care provider about findings and tracing and request bedside urgent evaluation?

Answer: Yes - Jane's primary provider Dr. Clark should be notified about the following: vaginal examination status, SROM with thick meconium fluid present, EFM tracing characteristics and the lack of fetal response to scalp stimulation. During this communication the nurse should clearly request an urgent bedside evaluation. In some situations, it is optimal for the clinician to remain at the bedside and request that someone else notify the primary care provider to request a bedside evaluation. The immediacy of the request is determined by the clinical situation. Some situations may require changes in the plan of care while others require bedside evaluation.

19) Notify the charge nurse?

Answer: Yes - Advise the charge nurse of Jane’s situation and that an urgent bedside consult by the primary care provider has been requested. The change nurse can coordinate or assist with coordination of the perinatal team, if needed. She may also be able to assist with or instruct another staff member to assist with interventions, as needed.

20) Support maternal coping?

Answer: Yes - Calmly explain interventions to Jane as they are provided in an effort to enhance her understanding of the situation and to decrease potential anxiety. Explain to Jane that her induction will be delayed until the primary provider is able to evaluate her.
