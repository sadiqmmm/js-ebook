Case Study 2: Tammy

Tammy is a 25 year old, gravida 3, para 2002 at 37 5/7 weeks gestation. Two previous pregnancies resulted in spontaneous vaginal births at 38 6/7 weeks gestation and 39 4/7 weeks gestation. She is obese (pre-pregnancy BMI 36) with no other relevant medical history. Surgical history includes an appendectomy at age 10. The father of this baby is different than the father of Tammy's other two children, and he is curently deployed overseas. Tammy's current obstetric history: weight gain of 56 lbs with this pregnancy; mild preeclampsia; 2nd trimester ultrasound consistent with the last menstrual period (LMP) and normal anatomy; and 3rd trimester ultrasounds show normal growth.

Tammy presented to labor and delivery at 0600 for a scheduled medically indicated induction of labor for mild preeclampsia. Vaginal examination (VE) on admission revealed a cervix that was 2 cm dilated, 90% effaced, and a fetal vertex at 0 station. Her vital signs were as follows: temperature oral: 98.7° F (37.0° C); blood pressure: 154/97 mmHg; pulse: 80 bpm; and respirations: 20 breaths per minute. The nurse placed external EFM components, and interpreted the admission FHR tracing as having Category I characteristics with uterine contractions every 2-6 minutes, mild to palpation. Oxytocin was initiated at 0730 at 2 mu/min per protocol and provider's orders. At 0800, the oxytocin was increased to 4 mu/min. Tammy's physician performed artificial rupture of membranes with clear fluid at 0817. Vaginal examination at 0817 revealed the cervix was 3-4 cm dilated, 90% effaced, and the fetal head was at 0 station. This tracing begins at 0940 over two hours after the oxytocin was initated.

Identify the FHR characteristics evident in the first 7 minutes of Tammy’s tracing (0940-0947):

1) What is the baseline fetal heart rate?

Answer: 155 - Approximate mean FHR rounded to increments of 5 bpm during a 10-minute window excluding accelerations and decelerations and periods of marked variability. There must be ≥ 2 minutes of identifiable baseline segments.

2) What is the variability?

Answer: Minimal - Amplitude range (>0 but ≤ 5 bpm).

3) Are accelerations present?

Answer: No.

4) What is the frequency of the contractions?

Answer: 2 minutes - Contraction frequency is measured from the beginning of one contraction to the beginning of the next and is expressed in minutes.

5) What is the duration of the contractions?

Answer: 60-80 seconds - Duration is the length of the contraction and is described in seconds.

6) What is the category?

Answer: Category II, indeterminate - Category 2 tracing- the variability is minimal, and decelerations are present.

7) What type of periodic change occurred?

Answer: Variable decelerations.

What clinical interventions and communications are appropriate in the presence of these tracing characteristics and the assessment information about Tammy?

9) Is internal fetal monitoring needed (FSE or IUPC)?

Answer: No - The external monitor is recording an interpretable fetal tracing that can be evaluated.

10) Turn the mother to a lateral position?

Answer: Yes - When the mother is in a lateral position (right or left) the uterus does not compress the vena cava or aorta; thus, maternal cardiac return and cardiac output are maximized, and blood flow to the uterus is optimal. Changing positions may also alleviate cord compression, if present.

11) Fluid bolus of at least 500 mL of lactated Ringers solution if not contraindicated?

Answer: Yes - A fluid bolus increases maternal blood volume and uteroplacental perfusion and is not contraindicated.

12) Administer supplemental oxygen at 10 L/min using non-rebreather mask and discontinue as soon as possible?

Answer: No - First determine if repositioning the mother, administering a fluid bolus, and discontinuing the oxytocin improves the tracing. If oxygen is needed, it should be discontinued when the tracing improves.

13) Decrease or discontinue oxytocin?

Answer: Yes - There are decelerations and the variability has decreased.

14) Administer tocolytic (e.g., terbutaline)?

Answer: No - Tocolytics may be administered as a temporary intrauterine resuscitation measure to reduce uterine activity when tachysystole occurs.

15) Perform fetal scalp stimulation?

Answer: No - Not at this time. Fetal scalp stimulation is an indirect method used to evaluate oxygenation and acid-base status but is not indicated at this time. An acceleration is associated with fetal-well-being; however, absence of acceleration does not predict fetal compromise. Fetal scalp stimulation is performed during segments of baseline, not during decelerations.

16) Monitor maternal vital signs for hypotension?

Answer: Yes - Hypotension decreases uteroplacental perfusion.

17) Administer a vasopressor (e.g., ephedrine)?

Answer: No - Ephedrine may be administered when a mother has hypotension secondary to regional analgesia or anesthesia.

18) An amnioinfusion may be ordered?

Answer: Yes - An amnioinfusion may be ordered however, the physician did not order this intervention, waiting for evaluation of the fetal tracing in 30 minutes. Amnioinfusion may be administered in an attempt to resolve variable decelerations. These decelerations are often caused by umbilical cord compression due to decreased amniotic fluid.

19) Notify primary care provider about findings and tracing?

Answer: Yes - The physician should be notified about the EFM tracing characteristics and that the oxytocin was discontinued. The physician viewed the tracing remotely.

20) Notify the charge nurse?

Answer: Yes - Advise the charge nurse about the fetal tracing and that the physician has been notified. The change nurse can coordinate or assist with coordination of the perinatal team, if needed. She may also be able to assist with or instruct another staff member to assist with interventions, as needed.

21) Support maternal coping?

Answer: Yes - Calmly explain interventions as they are provided in an effort to enhance her understanding of the situation and to decrease potential anxiety.

The RN discontinued the oxytocin and initiated intrauterine resuscitation measures including placing Tammy in a lateral position, and administering 500 mL fluid bolus of lactated Ringers. Vaginal examination (VE) revealed a cervix that was 6-7 cm dilated, 100% effaced, and a fetal vertex at 0 station.

Her vital signs were as follows: temperature oral: 98.6° F (37.0° C); blood pressure: 153/90 mmHg; pulse: 96 bpm; and respirations: 22 breaths per minute. The RN called the physician to notify her of the FHR pattern, the clinical interventions performed, and the maternal and fetal responses. The physician observed the pattern remotely from her office and requested that the RN restart the oxytocin. The RN stated that Tammy was in active labor and had achieved a cervical dilation of 6-7 cms. The RN and physician agreed to delay restarting the oxytocin and to re-evaluate the FHR pattern, maternal VS and labor progress in 30 min. After that time, the RN would call a status report to the physician and determination would be made about initiation of an amnioinfusion and restarting the oxytocin.

Tammy’s cervical exam at 1015 was 8 cm dilated, 100% effaced and +1 station. The FHR baseline was 150 bpm with minimal to moderate variability with recurrent periodic variable decelerations. The RN stated that Tammy’s labor was progressing rapidly and she requested the physician come for a bedside evaluation. The physician arrived to the unit and Tammy proceeded to a spontaneous vaginal birth at 1042. Apgars were 7 at 1 minute and 9 at 5 min. There was a nuchal cord X2 and was reduced at delivery of the head.
