Case Study 5: Waverly

Waverly is a 33 year old woman, G4P3 at 38 6/7 weeks gestation. Her previous pregnancies resulted in spontaneous vaginal births at 40 5/7 weeks gestation, 39 3/7 weeks gestation, and 40 weeks gestation.Waverly is obese; she is 64 inches tall and weighed 234 pounds before pregnancy. Waverly followed her primary care provider’s advice and gained 16 pounds during this pregnancy, within the recommended range of 11 to 20 pound weight gain for pregnant obese women. She uses CPAP for sleep apnea; there are no additional risk factors in her medical history. Surgical history includes an appendectomy at age 13 and repair of a deviated septum at age 15. Prenatal laboratory results are within normal limits.

Waverly presented to the labor and delivery unit at 1600, stating the contractions that started at 1100 were now more frequent and intense. Her vital signs were: temperature oral 97.8° F (36.5° C); blood pressure 136/84 mmHg; pulse 96 bpm; respirations, 24 breaths per minute. Waverly has a birth plan which includes her wishes to have no pain medications during labor. Her husband is a long-distance trucker whose return has been delayed for several days by a severe winter storm although he expects to home by 1900 this evening.

VE revealed a cervix that was 4 cm dilated, 90% effaced, and a fetal vertex at +1 station. Waverly stated her back really hurt during contractions. The nurse placed Waverly in a lateral position using a wedge. External monitoring was placed on Waverly's abdomen; however, neither the FHR nor the uterine contractions recorded very well. The nurse listened to the fetal heart with a hand-held Doppler and determined the FHR was 140 bpm; no decreases were heard. The nurse also palpated the uterine contractions: frequency, every 2-2 ½ minutes; duration, 60-70 seconds, and the intensity, moderate. The nurse called the primary OB provider to report Waverly's arrival, vital signs, and VE. She also reported the auscultated FHR and palpated uterine contractions. Waverly's provider requested Waverly be admitted for labor.

The nurse readjusted both the ultrasound transducer and tocodynamometer. The FHR was occasionally recorded, but too sporadically to interpret. The uterine contractions did record after several readjustments of the tocodynamometer and were consistent with the nurse's assessment by palpation. At 1650 the nurse listened to the FHR after a contraction; hearing a FHR of 125 increasing to 140. She reassessed the FHR after the next contraction and again heard a FHR of 125 increasing to 140. Waverly's primary OB provider arrived on the unit to assist another mother with birth. The nurse talked with the provider about the difficulty obtaining FHR data and they both agreed that a fetal spiral electrode should be placed. The provider discussed the plan with Waverly, who agreed to the FSE placement. The provider ruptured Waverly's membranes and placed the FSE at 1710. The VE revealed a cervix that was 5 cm dilated, 100% effaced, and a fetal vertex at +1 station. The amniotic fluid was clear and in moderate amount.

What clinical interventions and communications are appropriate in the presence of these tracing characteristics and the assessment information about Waverly?

9) Is internal fetal monitoring needed (FSE or IUPC)?

Answer: No - A FSE was placed. The tocodynamometer was readjusted and contractions were recorded so that the fetal tracing is now interpretable. An interpretable fetal tracing is important for the assessment of fetal status so that the need for uterine resuscitation can be determined. The least invasive mode of monitoring should be used. External monitoring should be adjusted to obtain an interpretable tracing; internal monitoring is generally not needed. Administration of an amnioinfusion requires an IUPC.

10) Change the mother's position?

Answer: Yes - When the mother is in a lateral position (right or left) or another non-supine position the uterus does not compress the vena cava or aorta; thus, maternal cardiac return and cardiac output are maximized, and blood flow to the uterus is optimal. Changing positions may also alleviate cord compression, if present.

11) Fluid bolus of at least 500 mL of lactated Ringers solution if not contraindicated?

Answer: No - The fetal tracing is normal. There are data to suggest that increasing IV fluids will positively affect uterine blood flow and thus fetal oxygenation, even in women who are normotensive and well hydrated.

12) Administer supplemental oxygen at 10 L/min using non-rebreather mask and discontinue as soon as possible?

Answer: No - The fetal tracing is normal. This intervention is used if other interventions such as position change and/or fluid bolus do not improve the tracing. Oxygen is discontinued as soon as possible based on fetal response.

13) Decrease or discontinue oxytocin?

Answer: No - Oxytocin is not being administered at this time. Oxytocin may be decreased or discontinued to reduce uterine activity. When oxygen is chosen for intrauterine resuscitation, it is assumed that other sources of potential fetal physiologic stress have been minimized; thus, oxytocin should not be infusing concurrently with maternal oxygen administration.

14) Administer tocolytic (e.g., terbutaline)?

Answer: No - Tocolytics may be administered as a temporary intrauterine resuscitation measure to reduce uterine activity.

15) Perform fetal scalp stimulation?

Answer: No - The variability is moderate. Fetal scalp stimulation is an indirect method used to evaluate oxygenation and acid-base status. An acceleration is associated with fetal-well-being; however, absence of acceleration does not predict fetal compromise. Fetal scalp stimulation is performed during segments of baseline, not during decelerations.

16) Correct maternal hypotension?

Answer: No - Hypotension is not present at this time.Hypotension decreases uteroplacental perfusion which becomes readily apparent in the fetal heart rate.

17) Administer a vasopressor (e.g., ephedrine)?

Answer: No - Ephedrine may be administered when a mother has hypotension secondary to regional analgesia or anesthesia. Administration of vasopressors is generally addressed in protocols and requires a provider’s order.

18) Administer an amnioinfusion?

Answer: No - Amnioinfusion is not indicated in Waverly's current situation. Amnioinfusion may be administered in an attempt to resolve variable decelerations. These decelerations are often caused by umbilical cord compression due to decreased amniotic fluid.

19) Notify primary care provider about findings and tracing?

Answer: Yes - The provider should be notified about the EFM tracing characteristics. The tracing was not interpretable when the nurse first talked with the provider, and they jointly developed a plan for resolving the problem. In some situations, it is optimal for the clinician to remain at the bedside and request that someone else notify the primary care provider to request a bedside evaluation. The immediacy of the request is determined by the clinical situation. Some situations may require changes in the plan of care while others require bedside evaluation.

20) Notify the charge nurse?

Answer: Yes - Advise the charge nurse about Waverly's vital signs and the fetal tracing. The charge nurse can coordinate or assist with coordination of the perinatal team, if needed. She may also be able to assist with or instruct another staff member to assist with interventions, as needed.

21) Support maternal coping?

Answer: Yes - Calmly explain interventions as they are provided in an effort to enhance her understanding of the situation and to decrease potential anxiety.

At 1750 Waverly stated her pain was 7 on a pain scale of 10 and requested something for pain stating she did not want an epidural. The nurse administered butorphanol 2 mg IV push slowly at 1758. At 1810 Waverly stated that her pain was 3 on a pain scale of 10.Waverly's husband arrived.

The FHR baseline was 140; variability moderate; early decelerations, and accelerations present. Contractions were 3 minutes apart; duration, 90 secs; and moderate by palpation. Waverly stated she still felt back pain, so the nurse assisted Waverly to her hands and knees at 1817, and then to a sitting position at 1840; and continued to alternate between these two positions while providing counter-pressure and other supportive techniques.

Waverly had the urge to push at 1948. A VE revealed a cervix completely dilated and fetal vertex at +2 station. Her baby born was born at 2020, Apgar scores 8 & 9 at 1 & 5 minutes. Waverly's baby was placed skin to skin with her and readily breastfed before they were both transferred to the Mother-Baby Unit.
