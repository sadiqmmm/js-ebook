Case Study 4: Anisha

Anisha was a 23 year old woman, G1P0 at 38 1/7 weeks gestation. She has no significant medical history and has never had surgery. After preeclampsia was diagnosed at 34 0/7 weeks, Anisha was followed 2x a week in her primary care physician's office. She was transferred to the high risk unit of the referral center 36 hours ago from an outlying county due to increased diastolic blood pressure, edema, and proteinuria.

Anisha's diastolic blood pressures gradually increased from 102 on admission to 118 this morning despite bed rest and increasing her nifedipine dosage. She now reports having a severe frontal headache that she rates as a 10 on the 0 to 10 pain scale. Her urinary output was 60 mL in the past 3 hours. The nurse on the antenatal unit called the resident,
Dr. Bell, to request bedside evaluation, and he arrived within 15 minutes. Dr. Bell called the charge nurse on labor and delivery to request an induction as soon as possible.
Anisha was transferred to the labor and delivery unit. Anisha’s vaginal examination on admission to labor and delivery reveals she is 4 cm/100% effaced/0 station with intact membranes. An external fetal monitor is placed at 1042, and the nurse immediately notes that Anisha is having uterine contractions.

Only respond to these questions in relation to the segments of Anisha’s tracing between (1044-1054):

1) What is the baseline fetal heart rate?

Answer: 145 - Approximate mean FHR rounded to increments of 5 bpm during a 10-minute window excluding accelerations and decelerations and periods of marked variability. There must be ≥ 2 minutes of identifiable baseline segments.

2) What is the variability?

Answer: Moderate - Amplitude range (6–25 bpm).

3) Are accelerations present?

Answer: No.

4) What is the frequency of the contractions?

Answer: 2-2 ½ minutes - Contraction frequency is measured from the beginning of one contraction to the beginning of the next and is expressed in minutes.

5) What is the duration of the contractions?

Answer: 60-70 seconds - Duration is the length of the contraction and is described in seconds.

6) What is the category?

Answer: Category II, indeterminate - Category 2 tracing decelerations are present.

7) What type of periodic change occurred?

Answer: Late decelerations.

What clinical interventions and communications are appropriate in the presence of these tracing characteristics and the assessment information about Anisha?

9) Is internal fetal monitoring needed (FSE or IUPC)?

Answer: No - The external monitor is recording an interpretable fetal tracing. An interpretable fetal tracing is important for the assessment of fetal status so that the need for uterine resuscitation can be determined. The least invasive mode of monitoring should be used. External monitoring should be adjusted to obtain an interpretable tracing; internal monitoring is generally not needed. Administration of an amnioinfusion requires an IUPC.

10) Change the mother's position?

Answer: Yes - When the mother is in a lateral position (right or left) or another non-supine position the uterus does not compress the vena cava or aorta; thus, maternal cardiac return and cardiac output are maximized, and blood flow to the uterus is optimal. Changing positions may also alleviate cord compression, if present.

11) Fluid bolus of at least 500 mL of lactated Ringers solution if not contraindicated?

Answer: No - Since a fluid bolus increases maternal blood volume and Anisha has severe preeclampsia, the nurse should confer with physician before administering any bolus.

12) Administer supplemental oxygen at 10 L/min using non-rebreather mask and discontinue as soon as possible?

Answer: No - The nurse should determine if the position change improves the tracing. This intervention is used if other interventions such as position change and/or fluid bolus do not improve the tracing. Oxygen is discontinued as soon as possible based on fetal response.

13) Decrease or discontinue oxytocin?

Answer: No - Oxytocin is not being administered at this time. Oxytocin may be decreased or discontinued to reduce uterine activity. When oxygen is chosen for intrauterine resuscitation, it is assumed that other sources of potential fetal physiologic stress have been minimized; thus, oxytocin should not be infusing concurrently with maternal oxygen administration.

14) Administer tocolytic (e.g., terbutaline)?

Answer: No - Tocolytics may be administered as a temporary intrauterine resuscitation measure to reduce uterine activity.

15) Perform fetal scalp stimulation?

Answer: No - The variability is moderate. Fetal scalp stimulation is an indirect method used to evaluate oxygenation and acid-base status. An acceleration is associated with fetal-well-being; however, absence of acceleration does not predict fetal compromise. Fetal scalp stimulation is performed during segments of baseline, not during decelerations.

16) Correct maternal hypotension?

Answer: Yes - Hypotension decreases uteroplacental perfusion which becomes readily apparent in the fetal heart rate. Hypotension in this instance would be relative to the trending high blood pressures that in other cases would be considered normal.

17) Administer a vasopressor (e.g., ephedrine)?

Answer: No - Ephedrine may be administered when a mother has hypotension secondary to regional analgesia or anesthesia.

18) Administer an amnioinfusion?

Answer: No - There are no variable decelerations in this tracing. Amnioinfusion may be administered in an attempt to resolve variable decelerations. These decelerations are often caused by umbilical cord compression due to decreased amniotic fluid.

19) Notify primary care provider about findings and tracing?

Answer: Yes - The physician should be notified about the EFM tracing characteristics. In some situations, it is optimal for the clinician to remain at the bedside and request that someone else notify the primary care provider to request a bedside evaluation. The immediacy of the request is determined by the clinical situation. Some situations may require changes in the plan of care while others require bedside evaluation.

20) Notify the charge nurse?

Answer: Yes - Advise the charge nurse about Anisha’s vital signs and the fetal tracing. The charge nurse can coordinate or assist with coordination of the perinatal team, if needed. She may also be able to assist with or instruct another staff member to assist with interventions, as needed.

21) Support maternal coping?

Answer: Yes - Calmly explain interventions as they are provided in an effort to enhance her understanding of the situation and to decrease potential anxiety.

The nurse initiated an IV via a volume control pump. Dr. Bell was at the bedside and ordered infusions of Labetalol and magnesium sulfate which will be prepared by the pharmacy.

The nurse reviewed the fetal tracing between 1054 and 1110. The FHR baseline remained 145 bpm; the variability, moderate; accelerations x2, and no further decelerations. Contraction frequency was 2-4 minutes; duration, 50–90 seconds; intensity, mild on palpation; and resting tone soft on palpation between contractions. The interpretation is normal or Category 1 at this time. Dr. Bell orders an induction and discusses the plan of care with Anisha. The nurse and Dr. Bell discuss the plan care (to include DIC lab studies), including goals, parameters, and timelines for reassessment and notification.

The nurse will continue the external fetal monitoring, position Anisha in a lateral position, carefully titrate the Labetalol and oxytocin infusions per Dr. Bell’s order and unit protocol, and continue the magnesium sulfate. Anisha's vital signs and DTR will be assessed as well as her headache, using a pain scale. Intake and output will be monitored hourly. The nurse will keep her documentation up-to-date to assist in assessing trends. Anisha’s nurse will communicate the plan care, including goals, parameters, and timelines for reassessment and notification to the charge nurse and other team members.

The nurse talks with Anisha to assess whether she understands the plan of care, answering questions and clarifying information. The nurse asks Anisha if she called her family, and suggests that Anisha ask them to come to the hospital if possible. Anisha’s family at her bedside may help alleviate her anxiety about the induction and being in a hospital distant from her home.
