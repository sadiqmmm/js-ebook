Case Study: April

April is a 28-year-old G1P0 who is 40 weeks gestation by LMP and first trimester ultrasound examination. She came to the labor unit complaining of irregular contractions. She denies vaginal discharge or leaking of fluid. She states her fetus is as active as usual and she has been performing fetal kick counts after meals since the prenatal visit with her primary care physician last week. She hopes she will be admitted because yesterday was her due date and she is tired of being pregnant.

April’s prenatal course was uncomplicated and she never missed any appointments. There were no abnormalities related to her prenatal laboratory studies. Her medical history reveals no risk factors; no prior surgeries. Her mother is present with her on arrival.

An electronic fetal monitor (EFM) was placed on April’s abdomen at 1030. She was positioned with a lateral tilt and oriented to the nurses caring for her as well as to the purpose for the EFM and about the information it will provide to her care providers. Her initial vital signs were: BP 119/72; pulse 72 bpm; respirations 19/min and temperature 98.6°; all within the same range as recorded on her prenatal record.

April’s nurse left the room to answer a telephone call and after leaving, April moved into the supine position because the contractions she was experiencing seemed more intense to her while she was on her side.

April’s nurse notes the tracing at 1052 from the central telemetry station and returns immediately to April's room.

Identify the FHR characteristics evident in the first 7 minutes of April's tracing (1051-1059):

1) What is the baseline fetal heart rate?

Answer: 150 - Approximate mean FHR rounded to increments of 5 bpm during a 10-minute window excluding accelerations and decelerations and periods of marked variability. There must be ≥ 2 minutes of identifiable baseline segments.

2) What is the variability?

Answer: Minimal - Amplitude range (>0 but ≤ 5 bpm).

3) Are accelerations present?

Answer: No.

4) What is the frequency of the contractions?

Answer: 2-2 ½ minutes - Contraction frequency is measured from the beginning of one contraction to the beginning of the next and is expressed in minutes.

5) What is the duration of the contractions?

Answer: 60-80 seconds - Duration is the length of the contraction and is described in seconds.

6) What is the category?

Answer: Category II, indeterminate - Category II tracing decelerations are present.

7) What type of periodic change occurred?

Answer: Late decelerations.

8) Which physiologic goals need to be addressed?

Physiologic goals for observed pattern:


Answers:
Maximize uteroplacental blood flow
Maximize oxygenation
Maximize umbilical circulation
Support maternal coping

What clinical interventions and communications are appropriate in the presence of these tracing characteristics and the assessment information about April?

9) Is internal fetal monitoring needed (FSE or IUPC)?

Answer: No - The external monitor is recording an interpretable fetal tracing that can be evaluated.

10) Turn the mother to a lateral position?

Answer: Yes - When the mother is in a lateral position (right or left) the uterus does not compress the vena cava or aorta; thus, maternal cardiac return and cardiac output are maximized, and blood flow to the uterus is optimal. Changing positions may also alleviate cord compression, if present.

11) Fluid bolus of at least 500 mL of lactated Ringers solution if not contraindicated?

Answer: No - In this case, the nurse should determine if the position tracing improved the tracing.

12) Administer supplemental oxygen at 10 L/min using non-rebreather mask and discontinue as soon as possible?

Answer: No - The nurse should determine if the position change improves the tracing.

13) Decrease or discontinue oxytocin?

Answer: No - Oxytocin is not being administered at this time.

14) Administer tocolytic (e.g., terbutaline)?

Answer: No - Tocolytics may be administered as a temporary intrauterine resuscitation measure to reduce uterine activity when tachysystole occurs.

15) Perform fetal scalp stimulation?

Answer: No - Not at this time. First use uterine resuscitation and reassess. Fetal scalp stimulation is an indirect method used to evaluate oxygenation and acid-base status. An acceleration is associated with fetal-well-being; however, absence of acceleration does not predict fetal compromise. Fetal scalp stimulation is performed during segments of baseline, not during decelerations.

16) Monitor maternal vital signs for hypotension?

Answer: Yes - Hypotension decreases uteroplacental perfusion.

17) Administer a vasopressor (e.g., ephedrine)?

Answer: No - Ephedrine may be administered when a mother has hypotension secondary to regional analgesia or anesthesia.

18) An amnioinfusion may be ordered?

Answer: No - Amnioinfusion may be administered in an attempt to resolve variable decelerations. These decelerations are often caused by umbilical cord compression due to decreased amniotic fluid.

19) Notify primary care provider about findings and tracing?

Answer: Yes - The physician should be notified about the EFM tracing characteristics.

20) Notify the charge nurse?

Answer: Yes - Advise the charge nurse about the fetal tracing and that the physician has been notified. The change nurse can coordinate or assist with coordination of the perinatal team, if needed. She may also be able to assist with or instruct another staff member to assist with interventions, as needed.

21) Support maternal coping?

Answer: Yes - Calmly explain interventions as they are provided in an effort to enhance her understanding of the situation and to decrease potential anxiety.

At 1058, April’s nurse moved her to the left lateral position with a pillow wedge to her back. She rechecked April’s blood pressure and it was 121/73. April denied any shortness of breath or feelings of faintness. The nurse also advised April and her mother about the rationale for the position change. The nurse remained at the bedside and noted changes in the characteristics of the EFM tracing between 1102 and 1120. FHR baseline rate is155 bpm; variability, moderate; accelerations, present; and no decelerations. Uterine contractions are every 5 minutes; duration, 60-70 seconds; and intensity, moderate on palpation.The intervention of turning April to a lateral position alleviated the pressure on her vena cava and increased her cardiac output thereby increasing blood flow and oxygenation to the uterus, placenta and fetus.

At 1116, April’s nurse performed a vaginal examination with findings of the cervix at 1½ cm dilation, thick and the fetal head ballottable. The nurse provided report to the care provider and because the fetal tracing continued to have moderate variability, no decelerations, and accelerations at 1130 removed the EFM components so that April could ambulate around the labor unit to return in one hour for reevaluation.
